Kenzo Website Redesign

Overview

Problem

Outdated websites are common for businesses that have been around for a long time. For restaurants specifically, it’s important to keep an up-to-date site to keep up with their competitors and to ensure that customers can easily find the information they are looking for regarding their establishment.

The Japanese ramen restaurant, Kenzo, falls under this category. Their outdated website makes it difficult for users to navigate and find what they are looking for.

Solution Overview

For this project, my team prioritized improving Kenzo's navigation, information hierarchy, and visual aesthetic. This was done by minimizing the information on the site as well as ensuring important aspects of the restaurant are easy to find.

Design Process

What's Being Done?

​Before diving straight to redesigning the website, we wanted to explore Kenzo's current website a bit more in terms of what content is included and how navigation works amongst its pages. We also looked at restaurants that are similar to Kenzo (other noodle and Asian restaurants) to see how their websites are designed. This gives us a better idea of how other restaurants present themselves online and can serve as inspiration for the redesign.

What We Found

Overall, we found that Kenzo's website fails to compare to other modern and easy-to-navigate sites. Kenzo's current website has an overwhelming amount of information that is presented in text form, whereas other restaurants decided to visually show its customers through photos and/or videos. A large amount of text also contributes to making it difficult to find valuable information, such as menu items, because it is buried among numerous subpages.

Potential Users

We spent some time considering the types of people that are likely to visit restaurant sites. By looking at potential customers, we can decide what we need to prioritize. We decided to focus on

  1. Navigation
  2. Information hierarchy
  3. Modern design

Wireframes

Each team member created a low-fidelity wireframe to visually show everybody their ideas for the redesign. Designing individually helped us brainstorm without being influenced by each other. Afterwards, we shared and critiqued all the wireframes, then compiled parts that we thought would suit the final design.

Final Design

The final design was done with HTML and CSS and can be accessed through the link below.

View Kenzo Redesign

Overall, this project was pretty successful. We managed to improve on the three main points in our redesign, those being navigation, information hierarchy, and visual aesthetic. The navigation bar was simplified into four main categories (home, menu, about, and locations) so users do not have to dig through multiple subpages to find what they are looking for. We also opted for a more modern and minimalistic design for the redesign, which allowed for a consistent san-serif font, accent colour, and more whitespace.

Points of Consideration

Responsiveness
  • Is this website responsive across a variety of devices?
  • Are the buttons and links large enough on mobile devices? Or are they difficult to click?
Design consistency
  • Text size and spacing are inconsistent on certain pages. Next time, we can use platforms such as GitHub that allows us to work on the code efficiently (because changes will be synced right away).

My Takeaways

Less is more

Being able to explain something in a couple of sentences rather than paragraphs makes it easier for users to find and understand your message. This can apply to restaurant websites or even a personal portfolio!

Research what others are doing

It can be helpful to take a look at what other people or companies are doing in regards to your project topic. This gives you time to brainstorm what you like and dislike. For this project, taking a look at other restaurant sites definitely inspired the team in terms of how we wanted to visually communicate our information.
